How temperature affects solubility

    For most solid solutes in liquids, higher temperatures increase solubility because the added heat provides energy for breaking intermolecular bonds.
    Molecules of the solvent move faster, creating more interactions with the solute.
    Increased kinetic energy in hot water: In hot water, the water molecules move more quickly and collide with the salt crystals more often and with more energy. These collisions help break apart the ionic bonds in the salt (e.g., sodium chloride, NaCl), causing it to dissolve more readily.
    Higher solubility at higher temperatures: For most salts, solubility increases as temperature rises. This is because the dissolution process often requires energy to break the ionic bonds in the salt and disrupt the water's hydrogen bonds. Hot water provides this energy more effectively than cold water.
    Faster diffusion: In hot water, once the salt dissolves, the ions (like Na⁺ and Cl⁻) diffuse more quickly throughout the solution because the higher temperature gives them more kinetic energy.
    Cold water dissolves salt too: Salt can dissolve in cold water, just more slowly and in smaller amounts for most salts. This is because the water molecules in cold water move slower and provide less energy to separate the salt’s ions.
